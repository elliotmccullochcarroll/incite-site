How InCite Works

InCite is a real-time uncertainty detection layer for AI-generated text. This document explains the methodology behind the signal detection system, the scoring model, and the design decisions that shaped the tool.

Overview

InCite operates as a Chrome extension that intercepts Claude's responses as they stream into the page. Each sentence is sent to a signal analysis pipeline — a combination of deterministic pattern matching and a lightweight LLM call — that scores the sentence across nine risk dimensions. The result is a colored underline directly in Claude's UI, with a tooltip that breaks down exactly which signals fired and why. For citation, medical, and legal signals, a live Perplexity search can be triggered directly from the tooltip to fact-check the specific claim against real sources.

The core insight is that AI hallucinations are not random. They cluster around specific linguistic and structural patterns: citations to studies that may not exist, specific statistics attributed to named organisations, medical dosages that sound plausible but are subtly wrong, legal statutes that are slightly misquoted. InCite targets these patterns specifically rather than trying to fact-check every sentence.

The problem

Large language models generate text that is syntactically fluent and semantically coherent, which makes errors hard to spot without domain knowledge. The failure modes are well-documented: fabricated citations appear in roughly 65% of cases where a model generates a specific author-year-journal reference. Medical dosage figures are incorrect in approximately 40% of cases. Legal statute citations frequently contain errors of jurisdiction, numbering, or interpretation.

The deeper issue is that confident-sounding output is not correlated with accuracy. A model will state an invented statistic in the same tone it uses for established facts. InCite does not attempt to solve this at the model level — instead it exposes the structural risk to the user so they can make an informed decision about whether to verify before acting.

Design constraint: InCite is not a fact-checker. It cannot determine whether a specific claim is true or false. It identifies sentence structures that have a high prior probability of error and surfaces them for human review.

Signal types

Nine signals are defined, each targeting a distinct failure mode. Each signal has an independent weight that contributes to the combined uncertainty score.

Signal What it detects Risk basis
Citations Author–year–journal references, DOIs, study names Models fabricate specific citations at high rates. The more specific the reference, the higher the risk.
Medical Drug names, dosages, clinical claims, trial data Dosage errors and fabricated trial outcomes present direct harm risk. Error rate ~40% for specific figures.
Legal Statute citations, case law, regulatory conclusions Legal claims are jurisdiction-sensitive and frequently misquoted or cited from repealed provisions.
Entity stats Specific figures attributed to named organisations Revenue, headcount, founding dates for specific entities are commonly hallucinated when precise.
Superlatives "First", "only", "largest", "most" claims Require exhaustive world knowledge to verify. Models assert superlatives with unwarranted confidence.
Temporal Time-sensitive or version-specific claims Rates, rankings, software versions, and political facts change. Training cutoffs make these stale.
Hedging Model uncertainty markers: "I think", "might", "not certain" The model itself is signalling low confidence. A direct indicator of unreliable output.
Negated claims Factual negations: "does not cause", "no evidence" Negated claims are structurally harder to verify and are disproportionately represented in corrections.
Math Complexity formulas, approximations, unstated assumptions Mathematical claims often contain hidden assumptions or apply only under specific conditions.

Detection pipeline

Signal detection runs in two phases per sentence. Phase one is a fast, deterministic pass using regular expression patterns. Phase two is a targeted LLM call using Claude Haiku to handle signals that require semantic understanding.

Phase 1 — Pattern matching

Each signal type is defined by a set of regex patterns that target its characteristic linguistic structures. For example, the citation signal matches constructs like "according to [Author] et al.", "a [year] study published in [Journal]", and "[Author] and [Author] ([year])". The medical signal matches drug name patterns, dosage constructs ("[N]mg [frequency]"), and clinical terminology.

Pattern matching runs client-side in the extension before the API call, providing an immediate score. Each pattern match contributes a raw score to its signal. Multiple matches within the same signal compound the score up to a defined ceiling.

Phase 2 — LLM verification

Sentences that clear a minimum threshold in phase one are passed to Claude Haiku with a structured prompt asking it to score the sentence across the same signal dimensions. The Haiku response is a set of per-signal scores that are merged with the regex scores using a signal-specific weighting scheme. Signals that require semantic understanding receive higher LLM weight; signals where pattern matching is highly reliable receive lower LLM weight.

Sentence segmentation

Claude's responses are segmented into sentences before analysis. The segmentation algorithm handles common edge cases — abbreviations, decimal numbers, URLs, and code blocks — to avoid splitting mid-sentence. Code blocks are treated as a separate content type with a different signal set focused on API validity, deprecation risk, and logic completeness.

Fact-checking

For signals where a live source check adds meaningful value — citation, medical, and legal — InCite exposes a Live check (Perplexity) option inside the Verify dropdown on each signal card in the Advanced view. Clicking it sends the flagged sentence to the Perplexity sonar model, which searches the web in real time and returns a structured verdict.

Verdict taxonomy

The system prompt instructs the model to apply verdicts in strict order of precedence:

  • Contradicted — a named citation cannot be found, a specific number differs from what real sources report, or real sources directly state the opposite. A sentence that mixes correct general facts with a fabricated specific detail (wrong number, non-existent paper) is contradicted, not unverifiable.
  • Supported — the specific claim as stated, including any numbers, names, and citations, is confirmed by real sources.
  • Unverifiable — reserved for topics where no evidence exists either way: too obscure, too recent, or not yet indexed.

The result renders inline inside the signal card that triggered it, tinted with the card's own signal color. Each result includes a one-sentence verdict reason naming the specific detail that is confirmed or wrong, and up to three clickable source links with snippets.

Cost note: Each Perplexity check is a paid API call against the user's Perplexity API key. Checks are on-demand only — InCite never triggers them automatically.

Scoring model

Individual signal scores are combined into a single uncertainty score between 0 and 1. The combination is multiplicative rather than additive: each fired signal multiplies the base uncertainty upward. This reflects the real-world observation that co-occurring signals compound risk — a citation to a specific study about a drug dosage is riskier than either signal alone.

A small base uncertainty floor ensures that even a sentence with no fired signals retains a residual uncertainty — an acknowledgement that absence of signals does not guarantee accuracy. The score can be further modified by risk multipliers: compound signals like "citation + temporal" or "medical + legal" apply an additional multiplier when both constituent signals fire simultaneously.

When a user resolves a signal (marking it as checked), the signal's contribution is removed and the score is recomputed. This allows the uncertainty to decrease as the user works through the flags, giving a live indication of how much risk remains.

Certainty tiers

The continuous 0–1 score is mapped to four discrete tiers for display. The tiers are intentionally asymmetric — the "high" (clean) band is wide, while the "low" (high risk) band captures only the most severe combinations.

High
0 – 0.10
No meaningful signals detected. No underline shown.
Info
0.10 – 0.20
Minor signal. Worth noting but low priority to verify.
Medium
0.20 – 0.55
Meaningful risk. Verify before using in any consequential context.
Low
0.55 – 1.0
High risk. Multiple signals co-fired. Treat as unverified.

The minimum display tier is configurable in settings. Users who only want to see the highest-risk flags can set the threshold to "low", suppressing info and medium underlines. This is the default for the "Essential" preset.

Architecture

InCite is split across two components: a Chrome extension that runs in the browser and a lightweight Express API server that handles LLM calls.

Chrome Extension
content.js observes Claude's DOM, segments sentences, applies underlines and tooltip UI
→
Background Worker
background.js proxies API calls — bypasses HTTPS/HTTP mixed-content restrictions
→
Signal API
Express server runs regex phase 1, calls Claude Haiku for phase 2, returns scored segments
→
Claude Haiku
Lightweight model call for semantic signal scoring — fast and cost-efficient per sentence

✦ On-demand: Signal API also proxies Perplexity sonar for live fact-checks on citation, medical, and legal signals — triggered manually from the tooltip, not part of the automatic pipeline.

The API server is necessary because the extension runs in a Chrome content script context under https://claude.ai, which blocks mixed-content requests to a local HTTP server. The background service worker does not have this restriction and acts as a relay. In production, the API is deployed to a cloud host, making the extension self-contained from the user's perspective.

State persistence is handled via chrome.storage.local. Resolved signals are persisted by sentence fingerprint, so they survive page reloads and persist across sessions. User settings (preset, enabled signals, minimum tier) are also stored locally — no data leaves the browser except the sentence text sent to the analysis API.

Privacy note: The only data sent to the API is the text of individual sentences from Claude's response. No user identity, conversation history, or metadata is transmitted. API calls are made by the extension's background worker, not by any tracking infrastructure.

Design philosophy

Non-intrusive by default

InCite is designed to stay out of the way. Underlines only appear on sentences that clear the minimum threshold. The tooltip appears on hover and dismisses automatically. Nothing is injected into the conversation or Claude's input. The tool observes but does not intervene.

User-resolvable signals

Every flagged signal can be resolved by the user with a single click. This is a deliberate design choice: the tool's job is to surface risk, not to make judgements. Once a user has verified a claim independently, they can mark it resolved and the score updates accordingly. This gives users agency and avoids the tool becoming a source of noise over time.

Transparency over opacity

The Advanced view exposes the full signal breakdown with per-signal scores, evidence tags (the specific patterns that triggered each signal), and direct links to the appropriate verification sources. The goal is to make the tool's reasoning legible so users can calibrate their trust in it — and override it when appropriate.

Calibration over sensitivity

The signal weights and tier thresholds are deliberately conservative. It is better to miss a borderline case than to flag a clean sentence and erode the user's trust in the tool. False positives are treated as more damaging than false negatives. The "Standard" preset reflects this: all signals are enabled, but the minimum tier is set to "medium", suppressing the noisiest low-confidence flags.

Limitations

InCite is not a fact-checker and makes no claim to be. Several categories of error are explicitly out of scope:

Plausible falsehoods. A sentence that contains no structural risk signals but is factually wrong will not be flagged. InCite targets form, not truth. A model stating a false but ordinary-sounding claim ("The capital of Australia is Sydney") will pass through undetected.

Errors of omission. Missing context, incomplete caveats, and misleading framing are not detected. InCite flags what is present, not what is absent.

Domain calibration. Signal weights are calibrated for general knowledge tasks. Highly specialised domains — advanced mathematics, niche legal jurisdictions, rare medical conditions — may exhibit different error rates than the defaults assume.

Single model. InCite currently works only with Claude.ai. Different models have different hallucination profiles; the signal weights have not been validated for other systems. Support for additional platforms is planned.
